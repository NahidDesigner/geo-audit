PHASE 01

Access

FIXES: "AI crawlers allowed in robots.txt" · "CDN bot-blocking risk" · "Content readable without JavaScript"

If AI crawlers can't fetch the site, the client is permanently invisible to that engine. This is binary — there's no partial credit. It's also the highest-leverage fix you will ever make, and it takes ten minutes.

Unblock AI crawlers robots.txt

WordPress path: Yoast SEO → Tools → File Editor or RankMath → General Settings → Edit robots.txt. Replace any AI-bot Disallow blocks with this:

robots.txt 
# AI answer engines — allow
User-agent: GPTBot
Allow: /

User-agent: OAI-SearchBot
Allow: /

User-agent: ChatGPT-User
Allow: /

User-agent: ClaudeBot
Allow: /

User-agent: Claude-User
Allow: /

User-agent: PerplexityBot
Allow: /

User-agent: Perplexity-User
Allow: /

User-agent: Google-Extended
Allow: /

User-agent: *
Allow: /

Sitemap: https://CLIENTSITE.com/sitemap_index.xml
Ask the client — don't decide for them

Allowing GPTBot and Google-Extended also permits their content to be used for model training, not just retrieval. Most local businesses want the visibility and don't care. Some publishers object.

If they want retrieval without training: allow OAI-SearchBot and ChatGPT-User, disallow GPTBot. This reduces reach — tell them so plainly.

Fix Cloudflare the silent killer

Cloudflare changed its defaults to block AI crawlers. A site can allow every bot in robots.txt and still be blocked at the network layer. The owner has no idea. Your robots.txt fix does nothing if this isn't also fixed.

Cloudflare dashboard → Security → Bots → AI Scrapers and Crawlers → turn OFF "Block AI bots". Menu naming shifts; look for AI bot / AI Scrapers settings. Also check Security → WAF for custom rules blocking bot user-agents.

Verify it actually worked

terminal — expect HTTP/2 200, not 403 
curl -A "GPTBot" -I https://clientsite.com/
curl -A "ClaudeBot" -I https://clientsite.com/
curl -A "PerplexityBot" -I https://clientsite.com/

A 403 means still blocked — go back to Cloudflare. Screenshot the 200 responses; it's excellent proof-of-work for the client.

JavaScript rendering

Most AI crawlers do not execute JavaScript. If content only exists after JS runs, it's invisible. Standard WordPress/Elementor passes fine. Usual culprits when it fails: a JS-driven tab/accordion hiding real content, or aggressive lazy-load.

terminal — is the headline in the raw HTML? 
curl -s https://clientsite.com/ | grep -i "your headline text"

No match = AI can't see it. Fix by ensuring content renders server-side.

sameAs is quietly one of the most valuable fields — it's how AI corroborates that this business is a real entity existing across multiple platforms. Include Google Business Profile, Facebook, LinkedIn, Yelp, industry directories.

FAQPage schema highest ROI for GEO

FAQ markup maps exactly onto how people prompt AI. Someone types a question; the engine looks for a source that has that question with a clean answer under it. Add an FAQ block to every service page.

JSON-LD — FAQPage 
<script type="application/ld+json">
{
  "@context": "https://schema.org",
  "@type": "FAQPage",
  "mainEntity": [
    {
      "@type": "Question",
      "name": "How much does duct cleaning cost in Melbourne?",
      "acceptedAnswer": {
        "@type": "Answer",
        "text": "Duct cleaning in Melbourne typically costs between $199 and $499, depending on the number of vents. A standard 3-bedroom home with 8 vents averages $299. Pure Air charges a flat $299 for homes up to 10 vents, with no call-out fee."
      }
    },
    {
      "@type": "Question",
      "name": "How often should ducts be cleaned?",
      "acceptedAnswer": {
        "@type": "Answer",
        "text": "Most Melbourne homes need duct cleaning every 2 to 3 years. Homes with pets, smokers, or allergy sufferers benefit from annual cleaning. Ducts that have never been cleaned in 5+ years typically show significant dust accumulation."
      }
    }
  ]
}
</script>
Schema must mirror visible text

Never put answers in the JSON that don't appear on the page. That's cloaking, and it gets penalized. Write the FAQ section on the page first, then mirror it into schema.

Article schema, sitemap, canonicals

Yoast/RankMath handle these automatically. Just verify: Article schema has author, datePublished, and especially dateModified (freshness signals depend on it), and the sitemap exists at /sitemap_index.xml and is referenced in robots.txt.

The core rewrite pattern

Take each cornerstone page and restructure it. This transformation is what you're selling.

Before — uncitable

At Pure Air, we pride ourselves on delivering exceptional duct cleaning services to our valued customers throughout the Melbourne region. Our dedicated team of professionals brings years of industry experience and a commitment to excellence that sets us apart from the competition, ensuring your home's air quality is the very best it can be.

No fact. No number. No answer to any question. There is nothing here an AI can lift. It is invisible.

After — citable

How much does duct cleaning cost in Melbourne?

Duct cleaning in Melbourne costs $199–$499 depending on vent count. Pure Air charges a flat $299 for homes with up to 10 vents, with no call-out fee.

HomeVentsPrice
ApartmentUp to 5$199
Standard6–10$299
Large11+$399+

How long does duct cleaning take?

A standard clean takes 2–4 hours. In 2025, 78% of Pure Air jobs were completed in under 3 hours.

"The dust difference was visible within a day — my daughter's asthma symptoms dropped noticeably," said Sarah M., a Brunswick customer, in a March 2026 review.

See the EPA's guidance on indoor air quality for background.

Last updated: July 2026

Same information. What changed structurally: question-phrased H2s · direct answer in the first 1–2 sentences · concrete numbers · a table · an attributed quote · an outbound authority link · a visible date. That's every Phase 3 check satisfied in one page.

The rules, applied to every page

  1. Answer-first. Under every question heading, the direct answer goes in the first 40–60 words. Don't build up to it — AI lifts the top of the section.
  2. Question headings. "Our Services" → "What duct cleaning services do we offer?" Use the phrasing real customers use.
  3. Numbers over adjectives. "Fast service" → "Same-week booking; 78% of jobs done in under 3 hours." Every vague claim becomes a specific one.
  4. Short paragraphs. 2–4 sentences, one idea each. Long paragraphs don't get lifted cleanly.
  5. Lists and tables for anything comparable — pricing, service areas, process steps.
  6. One attributed quote minimum per cornerstone page. Owner, technician, customer review, industry expert. Attribution matters.
  7. Link out to authorities. .gov, .edu, industry bodies, research. Counterintuitive but proven: citing sources makes you more citable.
  8. Visible date stamps. "Last updated: July 2026". AI has a strong recency bias. Refresh quarterly.

Target the fan-out queries

When someone asks AI a long question, the engine breaks it into smaller sub-queries and searches each separately. "Best duct cleaner in Melbourne for a house with pets" fans out into "duct cleaning Melbourne," "duct cleaning pet dander," "duct cleaning cost." The client needs content answering each fragment.

Practically: ask the owner "what do customers actually ask you on the phone?" — those exact phrasings become your H2s. This is also exactly what PSEO Studio already generates at scale. The two products fit together: PSEO generates the page inventory, GEO makes each page citable.

Off-site — the part you must be honest about

Tell clients this instead of hiding it

The majority of AI citations come from earned media — third-party sources, not the brand's own website. On-site optimization is necessary but not sufficient.

Highest-leverage off-site moves, in order of accessibility:

  • Google Business Profile — fully filled, categories correct, Q&A populated, reviews answered. Free, fast, feeds local AI answers directly.
  • Reddit and YouTube — among the most-cited sources in AI answers. Genuine participation gets cited.
  • Directories & citations — Yelp, Clutch, Avvo (legal), industry associations. Consistent NAP everywhere.
  • Wikipedia/Wikidata entity — larger brands only. Don't promise this to a plumber.
  • Guest posts / press — slow and hard. Don't oversell.

Scope this honestly in your gig. You can do the first three. You cannot promise Forbes.

PHASE 04

Verify and prove

This is what converts a one-time gig into a retainer and a case study.

Re-run the audit

Same tool, same site. The before/after score is the deliverable that justifies the invoice: "Your AI visibility score went from 34/100 (F) to 88/100 (B+)." Screenshot both.

Manual citation testing worth more than any score

Actually go ask the engines. Open ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude, and Google AI Mode, and run the client's real customer questions:

test prompts — screenshot the answers 
Who's the best duct cleaning company in Melbourne?
How much does duct cleaning cost in Melbourne?
Duct cleaning near Brunswick with good reviews
Do this BEFORE you start work

Screenshot the engines failing to mention the client during the audit phase. Then again 4–8 weeks after. That before/after pair sells your next three gigs.

Set the timeline honestly

Re-crawling and re-indexing takes time. Perplexity moves fastest (it favors recent content). ChatGPT can take weeks. Tell the client 4–8 weeks minimum before citation changes appear. Anyone promising instant AI ranking is lying — and if you promise it, you'll eat the refund.

Ongoing monitoring your retainer hook

Monthly: re-run the audit, re-test the citation prompts, check server logs for AI bot hits. Legitimate $99–$299/month recurring — and it's the same work every month, which is exactly what you want.

02
Schema/content mismatch.

Timeline promises.
